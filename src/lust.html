This quest for validation can lead users to prioritize their online personas over their authentic selves, perpetuating a cycle of comparison and insecurity. Furthermore, technology companies often employ persuasive design techniques, such as personalized recommendations and targeted advertisements, to exploit users' desires and preferences for profit.

These tactics can create a sense of craving or compulsion to engage with the platform, contributing to addictive behaviors and a lack of self-control. Additionally, the proliferation of sexualized content and objectification on social media can fuel lustful desires and unhealthy attitudes towards relationships and intimacy. Finally, the unchecked accumulation of data and power by technology companies can reflect a lust for control and dominance, as they seek to influence users' behavior and manipulate their personal information for financial gain. Overall, the sin of lust permeates social media and its ownership, manifesting in the pursuit of attention, validation, profit, and control at the expense of users' well-being and autonomy. Whether through curated personas on platforms like Instagram or immersive virtual worlds like Second Life, social media allows users to construct idealized versions of themselves and engage in escapist behaviors, satisfying their lust for escapism and self-gratification.

Social media platforms often leverage users' desire for attention by employing features like notifications, streaks, and badges to keep them engaged for longer periods. This constant craving for validation and acknowledgment can lead to addictive behaviors as individuals seek the next dopamine hit from likes and comments. Technology companies exhibit a form of lust by relentlessly pursuing profits through the collection and exploitation of user data. This data is often used to target users with personalized advertisements, tapping into their desires and preferences to drive consumer behavior.
